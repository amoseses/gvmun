The 17th BRICS Summit 2025: A Defining Moment for the Global South

How emerging economies came together in Rio de Janeiro to reshape global governance and challenge the traditional world order

By Asawar Aftab
July 15, 2025
8 min read
Home Publications Journalism BRICS Summit

The 17th BRICS Summit in Rio de Janeiro brought together leaders from emerging economies to reshape global governance. (File photo)

The 17th BRICS Summit, held in Rio de Janeiro, Brazil, on July 6–7, 2025, marked one of the most consequential gatherings in the bloc's history. Under the theme "Strengthening Global South Cooperation for More Inclusive and Sustainable Governance," leaders from Brazil, Russia, India, China, South Africa, and newly admitted members such as Indonesia, Egypt, Ethiopia, Iran, and the United Arab Emirates came together to reshape the contours of international politics.

A Historic Gathering in Rio de Janeiro

More than just a routine meeting, the summit highlighted the determination of emerging economies to play a decisive role in world affairs.

The Rio Declaration: A Blueprint for Fairer Governance

A major outcome was the adoption of the Rio Declaration, which reaffirmed BRICS' vision of a multipolar global order—fair, inclusive, and resistant to domination by a single power. Alongside it, a groundbreaking declaration on artificial intelligence urged the United Nations to develop ethical and inclusive AI governance frameworks. This dual focus revealed the bloc's commitment not only to reforming outdated international institutions but also to embracing twenty-first-century challenges such as AI and technological disruption.

Brazil's Leadership and the Six Pillars of Debate

Brazil's stewardship ensured the summit went beyond symbolism. With more than 100 preparatory meetings across the country, the agenda was organized around six major themes: global health, trade and finance, climate change, AI governance, security reform, and institutional strengthening. This thematic breadth underscored BRICS' evolution from a loose coalition into a forum that seeks to actively influence global governance. President Luiz Inácio Lula da Silva's emphasis on sustainability and fairness resonated with a Global South increasingly dissatisfied with the current world order.

Expansion: From Regional Bloc to Global Force

The summit also underscored BRICS' rising influence through its expanded membership. Indonesia's entry as the first Southeast Asian member, alongside new partners from Africa and the Middle East, reflected the bloc's reach across continents. Collectively, BRICS now represents more than half of the world's population and commands a significant share of global GDP. For observers, this expansion signaled the bloc's transition from an emerging coalition of rising economies to a central player in shaping the balance of global power.

Clash with the U.S.: "We Don't Want an Emperor"

No summit would be complete without geopolitical friction, and in Rio the sparks came from Washington. Former U.S. President Donald Trump threatened punitive tariffs on BRICS economies, a move that provoked strong pushback. Leaders condemned the threats as "mistaken and irresponsible," while Lula delivered the most memorable line of the summit: "We don't want an emperor." This remark crystallized BRICS' core philosophy: a rejection of hegemony in favor of sovereignty, equality, and multipolarity.

De-Dollarization Without a Single Currency

One of the most anticipated agenda items was the question of global financial sovereignty. While speculation about a unified BRICS currency dominated headlines, the summit clarified that no such project is currently underway. Instead, leaders focused on alternative payment systems and currency cooperation to reduce reliance on the U.S. dollar. Though gradual, these moves point toward a long-term vision of financial diversification, offering Global South nations more stability in the face of dollar-driven volatility.

What the Experts Are Saying

Analysts offered mixed assessments. The Stimson Center praised the summit for amplifying the Global South's collective voice at a time of fractured global governance. By contrast, the Atlantic Council cautioned that internal contradictions—ranging from economic disparities to geopolitical rivalries—could undermine the bloc's unity. Policy experts highlighted that Brazil's strong leadership helped deliver a coherent agenda, but sustaining momentum will depend on India, which assumes the chairmanship in 2026.

A Turning Point or Just Another Summit?

The Rio Summit should be seen as more than a diplomatic showcase. It represented a declaration of intent from emerging economies to demand greater equity in international institutions such as the IMF, World Bank, and UN Security Council. The emphasis on AI governance, climate action, and de-dollarization showed that BRICS is not merely critiquing the current system—it is offering alternatives. Still, whether this vision translates into real change will depend on the bloc's ability to turn words into coordinated action.

Looking Ahead: India's Turn at the Helm

As the chairmanship passes to India in 2026, the international community will be watching closely. India's leadership is expected to emphasize sustainable energy and digital transformation, themes aligned with its national priorities. Yet it will also have to balance its delicate relationship with China and its ties to Western powers. If BRICS can sustain the momentum from Rio, the summit may be remembered as the moment the bloc moved decisively from aspiration to action.

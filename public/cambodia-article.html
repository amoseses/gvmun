Tens of Thousands Flee Homes as Cambodia–Thailand Clashes Escalate

A deadly border conflict erupts between Cambodia and Thailand over contested territories near ancient temples, forcing mass evacuations and threatening regional stability

By GVMUN Editorial Team
July 30, 2025
7 min read
Home Publications Journalism Cambodia-Thailand Border Conflict

Tensions escalate at the Cambodia-Thailand border amid territorial disputes. (File photo)

There was an armed conflict on the border of Cambodia and Thailand in 2025 caused by a long-term dispute about the border territories. Cambodia and Thailand accused each other in violation of agreements and provocations. As a result of the conflict, there were many victims. But what really happened?

The Night of July 24: Deadly Clashes Erupt

The armed clashes occurred on the night of July 24 near the ancient temple Ta Muen Thom. It is located on the territory of Thailand, next to the territory contested by Cambodia. According to the Thai authorities, 12 citizens of the country were killed as a result: 11 civilians (including a teenager and eight year old child) and one military. In the video that appeared on the Internet there were explosions and shootouts. The authorities of Thailand decided to evacuate the citizens of four provinces on the border with Cambodia: Surin, Buriram, Sisaket and Ubon Ratchathani. Thailand later reported almost 190 thousand evacuated residents from the border provinces.

Mutual Accusations and Escalation

The parties accused each other of escalating the conflict. The Thai Army stated that it was Cambodian troops that opened fire near the temple Ta Muen Thom. However, the Cambodia Ministry of Defense claimed that the soldiers acted for self-defense and opened fire in response to shelling from Thailand. Cambodia also accused Thailand of using heavy weapons and applying an airstrike on its territory. The country's authorities called the Thai actions "cruel and illegal aggression" and "egregious violation of the UN Charter and the fundamental principles of international law." At the same time, Bangkok accused the Cambodia Army of shelling civil facilities, including hospitals. On Thursday morning, Thailand announced the closure of the border with Cambodia. The country's embassy in Phnom Penh demanded their citizens to leave Cambodia, and those who intended to visit her to transfer their trips.

Historical Context: A Century of Territorial Disputes

Bangkok and Phnom Penh have been disposing of the affiliation of the territories of the Prea Vihear and Ta Muen Thom temples for more than a hundred years. The total length of the dispute border is more than 800 kilometers, the region is sparsely populated, in a large part it is inaccessible and insufficiently cartographic. Many elements of the border have remained since the days of French colonial rule - from 1863 to 1953, Cambodia was a protectorate of France, while Thailand - at that time the Kingdom of Siam - formally remained independent. Over the 90 years, several mutual concessions of territories occurred, and as a result of which the actual border was changed several times. In addition, the border established by France in 1907 was later challenged by Thailand.

Economic and Political Ramifications

As the conflict escalated, Cambodia banned the import of fruits and vegetables from Thailand and banned the showing of Thai films. The work of many border checkpoints on the two countries' border was suspended or restricted. Just a day before the conflict escalated, several Thai soldiers were injured by mines. Thailand accused Cambodia of having deliberately planted the mines.

But the Thai public is wondering whether the border dispute is a pretext for secret deals between two countries in a disputed area where rich fossil fuel reserves are believed to be located. Critics say the government's inaction is only fueling public suspicions about the hidden interests of the Shinawatra family (Thailand) and the Khun family (Cambodia).

Regional Spillover and International Concerns

The Thai Armed Forces called the incident "warfare outside the rules" and called for its immediate cessation. Bangkok also claimed that Phnom Penh had deliberately organized the shelling of Laotian territory in order to discredit the Thai military. The Chinese agency Xinhua, citing a source in the Laotian army, reported that the latest disseminated information about a shootout between Laotian and Cambodian soldiers was not true. However, the agency cited data that as part of the clashes between Thailand and Cambodia from July 24 to 25, 10 artillery shells exploded on Laotian territory - whose, is not specified. Cambodia, in turn, denied the reports of its shells falling in Laos, noting that Laos had not made any accusations.

U.S. Mediation Efforts

On July 26, US President Donald Trump said that Thailand and Cambodia had agreed to meet "immediately" to work out a ceasefire, only after which Washington would resume trade negotiations with the two countries. "Both sides want an immediate ceasefire and peace. They also want to return to trade negotiations with the US, which we believe is impossible until the arm conflict stops," Trump wrote on Saturday on his "TRUTH Social" social media account.

Path to Ceasefire: Malaysian Mediation

The ceasefire between Thailand and Cambodia came into effect at midnight on July 29 after five days of clashes. The parties agreed on the ceasefire with the mediation of Malaysian Prime Minister Anwar Ibrahim (ASEAN Chairman), who provided the parties with a platform for negotiations. As a result, on July 28, Acting Prime Minister of Thailand Phumtham Wechayachai and Prime Minister of Cambodia Khun Manet held a meeting in Kuala Lumpur, where a new ceasefire agreement was reached.

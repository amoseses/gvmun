Starvation Surges in Gaza as Aid Fails to Meet Needs

Gaza faces a worst-case famine scenario as food and humanitarian supplies barely trickle into the enclave, leaving thousands of children with acute malnutrition

By Alisa Pomelnikova
August 15, 2025
9 min read
Home Publications Journalism Gaza Crisis

Gaza faces severe food shortages and humanitarian crisis as aid delivery remains inadequate. (File photo)

On July 29, an international hunger organization issued its starkest warning yet: Gaza is on the brink of a "worst-case famine scenario", battered by months of Israeli military assault and an unrelenting blockade.

The Unfolding Crisis

For nearly a year, food and humanitarian supplies have barely trickled into the enclave. The crisis reached a critical point in March, when Israel imposed a complete blockade in the midst of a truce with Hamas, effectively cutting off the last lifelines of aid to the Gaza Strip. According to the Integrated Food Security Phase Classification (IPC), the number of families facing extreme hunger doubled between May and July. Many areas have now crossed the critical threshold that signals famine.

By mid-July, Gaza City was registering malnutrition rates that met formal famine criteria. In just three months (April - mid-July), more than 20,000 children were hospitalized with acute malnutrition; over 3,000 were in life-threatening condition. Doctors are warning of a sharp rise in deaths among children under five. Projections show more than 71,000 children and 17,000 mothers will soon require urgent, specialized treatment for acute malnutrition.

Insufficient Aid and Failed Promises

Under mounting international pressure, the Israeli army has announced occasional "tactical pauses" to allow in aid convoys. But relief agencies warn that this volume is catastrophically insufficient to stop the looming famine. After partially easing the blockade in May, Israel allowed only minimal amounts of humanitarian goods to be delivered for several weeks — a lifeline so thin that it failed to reach many in need. Moreover, according to humanitarian organizations, new import regulations have only worsened the situation, causing logistical disruptions and delays in the delivery of food and medicine.

"With such limited aid, the humanitarian situation is spiraling," said Dr. Amjad Alshawa, head of the Palestinian NGO Network.

Israel's Defense and Disputed Claims

Israeli officials maintain that restrictions and a controversial new distribution system are necessary to prevent Hamas from seizing aid. Yet a classified U.S. government analysis submitted to the State Department reportedly found no evidence of systematic aid theft by Hamas. The Israel Defense Forces dismissed the report as "biased."

At an August 10 press conference, Prime Minister Benjamin Netanyahu again rejected allegations of famine as "exaggerated." While acknowledging "deprivation" in the enclave, he insisted: "If Israel had a policy of starvation, no one in Gaza would have survived two years of war."

Meanwhile, the United Nations and other international organizations disagree, warning that the current level of aid (limited truck deliveries and sporadic airdrops) is completely insufficient for Gaza's two million residents.

Dangerous and Inadequate Relief Methods

Airdrops, in particular, have drawn public anger for their inefficiency and danger. In one tragic incident, 14-year-old Muhannad Eid was killed when a humanitarian package fell on him. "This is humiliation from the air, not help," his brother, Mohammed, told reporters. "We've warned again and again about these unsafe, inhumane methods and repeatedly called for the safe and adequate delivery of aid through land crossings. We need protection."

Why More Food Alone Won't End the Crisis

Experts caution that with famine already gripping parts of Gaza, simply increasing food deliveries will not be enough. Over half the population is now in the "emergency" or "catastrophic" phase of hunger, where the body's systems begin to collapse. The medical complexity of treating such phases of acute malnutrition makes the situation especially dangerous.

"In the final stages of starvation, even water can trigger organ failure, and improper or too-rapid feeding can cause refeeding syndrome, which is deadly due to the sudden imbalance of electrolytes," explained Dr. Patel, a specialist in emergency nutrition. Dr. Noor Alamassi of Project HOPE added: "A bag of flour will not save anyone if it does not contain the essential nutrients people need." Recovery requires weeks or even months of careful medical supervision. Children need fortified milk, ready-to-use therapeutic foods, and constant monitoring of sodium and potassium levels in the blood.

But in reality, Gaza's healthcare system is already crippled by shortages of doctors, medicine, and equipment to provide such care. Aid workers warn that even if food supplies resume, without medical infrastructure, saving thousands of lives will be extremely difficult.

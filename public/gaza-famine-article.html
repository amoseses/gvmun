Gaza Crisis: Latest Developments and Escalations

An unprecedented humanitarian emergency unfolds as famine conditions worsen and press freedom comes under attack

Andrea Pérez Rodríguez

Journalism Intern

October 6, 2025
8 min read
9,127 views

Children in Gaza face severe malnutrition amid ongoing humanitarian crisis. (File photo)

The Gaza Strip is currently facing an unprecedented humanitarian emergency, with the Integrated Food Security Phase Classification (IPC) confirming famine, recent Israeli airstrikes killing journalists such as Al Jazeera's Anas al-Sharif, and an escalating conflict that is deepening civilian suffering. This crisis is unfolding in Gaza, where millions, particularly children, are trapped between bombardments, forced displacement, and extreme malnutrition, while those reporting from the ground face growing risks.

The situation is worsening by the moment, and with the lack of humanitarian aid due to the closure of the borders by the Israelis, this situation does not seem likely to improve any time soon.

IPC Declares Famine in Gaza City

In late August 2025, the Integrated Food Security Phase Classification (IPC) officially declared a famine in parts of Gaza City, marking a critical turning point in the humanitarian crisis. According to their findings, thousands of families are facing starvation, with children and women suffering the highest mortality rates, as more than 138 deaths linked to malnutrition were recorded in August 2025, including 25 children.

Projections warn that over 132,000 children under five could die from acute malnutrition by June 2026, as AP News has announced it and Save the Children. This report brought international attention to the severity of the situation, as the IPC's declaration is considered the global standard for classifying food insecurity and famine conditions.

Famine Alert: The IPC's official declaration of famine in parts of Gaza City represents the highest level of food insecurity classification, indicating that deaths, destitution, and extremely critical levels of acute malnutrition are evident among the population.

This statement, however, is contradicted by recent events, such as the attempt from Barcelona to deliver humanitarian aid by ship only a few days ago, which was forced to return to port after being denied entry. This incident illustrates the systematic obstruction of humanitarian assistance and the deliberate prevention of relief efforts for the Palestinian population.

Targeting of Journalists Raises Press Freedom Concerns

Shortly after the release of the famine report, Israeli airstrikes intensified across Gaza, including a strike that killed several journalists, among them Al Jazeera correspondent Anas al-Sharif. His death was particularly impactful because he had been documenting the dire humanitarian situation inside hospitals and refugee camps, and shortly before his death, he had recorded a final video message describing the suffering of civilians.

The targeting of journalists raised renewed concerns over press freedom and the safety of reporters in conflict zones, as media workers have increasingly been caught in crossfire or deliberately targeted since the beginning of the war, a clear manifestation of dictatorial practices, seeking not only to suppress independent reporting but also to manipulate narratives and exert control over international opinion.

Press Freedom Under Attack: The death of journalists like Anas al-Sharif represents a broader pattern of targeting media workers, undermining independent reporting and the public's right to information about the humanitarian crisis.

International Response and Divided Opinions

International organizations such as the United Nations and the World Food Programme have warned that Gaza is on the verge of humanitarian collapse, urging Israel to lift restrictions on aid deliveries and allow full humanitarian access. The UN Secretary-General described the IPC famine declaration as "a wake-up call to the international community."

At the same time, humanitarian NGOs like Médecins Sans Frontières stressed that hospitals are overwhelmed and lacking both food and medicine. Israeli officials, however, have rejected these claims, arguing, as reported by Haaretz, that the data is "incomplete and politically manipulated," while insisting that aid trucks continue to enter Gaza on a daily basis.

International responses to the crisis remain sharply divided. According to The Guardian, European Union representatives condemned attacks on journalists and demanded accountability, framing press freedom as a non-negotiable principle even during wartime. The United States, meanwhile, voiced concern over civilian casualties but refrained from directly criticizing Israel, focusing instead on calls for de-escalation.

In contrast, Arab League leaders, cited by Al Jazeera, accused Israel of committing systematic violations of international law and urged the implementation of international sanctions. For civilians in Gaza, however, the issue is one of survival rather than politics: testimonies published in The New York Times describe parents skipping meals so their children can eat, journalists fearing for their lives, and families in refugee camps speaking of a "sense of abandonment," exposing the apparent separation between political disputes and the lived reality on the ground.

Global Implications and Long-term Consequences

The crisis in Gaza matters globally because it raises urgent questions about human rights, humanitarian law, and freedom of the press. If the IPC-confirmed famine and the targeting of journalists are ignored, the consequences could reach far beyond Gaza: mass civilian deaths, regional instability, and the erosion of international norms protecting civilians and reporters in armed conflicts.

In the short term, further restrictions on aid and ongoing airstrikes are likely to accelerate the humanitarian collapse. At the same time, divisions among world powers over how to respond may compromise the collective response.

Regional Stability at Risk: The Gaza crisis threatens to destabilize the broader Middle East region, with potential consequences for international law, humanitarian norms, and the safety of civilians and journalists in conflict zones worldwide.

In addition, the crisis shows deeper problems in how the region is governed and how the international community handles conflicts. People struggle to access basic resources, and political disagreements hinder the search for quick solutions. In the long run, the region's stability will depend on not just ending the current fighting, but also on rebuilding infrastructure, holding those responsible accountable, and creating ways for dialogue that prevent future conflicts.

Without sustained and coordinated action, people will continue to suffer the most.

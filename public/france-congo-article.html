France Announces $2.5 Billion Congo Basin Forest Initiative at COP30

Historic investment aims to protect one of Earth's most critical carbon sinks and support 80 million people

Shravya Nadagowda

Journalism Intern

December 27, 2025
9 min read

The Congo Basin rainforest, known as "the lungs of Africa," is the focus of a new $2.5 billion protection initiative

At COP30 in Belem, Brazil, France's European Nations announced a large $2.5 billion initiative towards protecting the Congo Basin rainforest, known as one of the most critical carbon sinks. This investment plays an essential role in safety. The Congo Basin, which has six Central African nations, stores excessive amounts of carbon, with varying rainfall patterns across the continent, and relies on sustainability to support over 80 million individuals. This investment can affect many fields of life, such as agriculture, mining, conflict, and governance.

The Overlooked "Lungs of Africa"

The Congo Basin was very overlooked at times, even being the second-largest rainforest on Earth, and a crucial, vital carbon sink. With the land affecting homes of over 130 million people in 6 countries across central Africa, the basin was referred to as "the lungs of Africa." At the COP30 Climate conference, the panel on science for the Congo Basin discussed the rainforest's consumption of 600 million tons of carbon dioxide, a figure that is deteriorating due to the acceleration of deforestation. The scientists believed the Congo was funded at a lower rate than its neighboring locations.

Between the dates of 2008 and 2022, Germany was the largest contributor to Central Africa, accounting for 24% of the total aid, with the Global Environment Fund at 12%, the World Bank at 9.4%, and the United States at 8.8%. A wide range of funding, 30%, was allocated to biodiversity conservation, with 27% going towards environmental policy. However, only 0.1% was directed toward scientific research, challenging the capacity of biodiversity and carbon.

Critical Carbon Sink: The Congo Basin absorbs 600 million tons of carbon dioxide annually, making it one of Earth's most vital natural climate regulators. However, accelerating deforestation threatens to transform this carbon sink into a carbon source.

The Financial Package and Implementation Strategy

French diplomats believed the launch of this initiative would complement existing mechanisms, such as the Global Environment Facility and the Central African Forest Initiative. The financial package, totaling $2.5 billion, consists of concessional loans from the Agence française de développement and funding grants from the United Kingdom, Germany, Norway, and Belgium. Approximately 60% of the funds will be allocated to performance-based roles for measurable deforestation reductions, accompanying a model very close to Norway's collaboration with Brazil.

The remaining funds will be spent toward community-driven projects on forestry, climate-smart agriculture, and strengthening protected area management, with a minimum of $300 million reserved for projects introduced by Congolese public institutions, reinforcing national climate priorities, prerequisites, and requirements. French diplomats believed the launch of this initiative would complement existing mechanisms, such as the Global Environment Facility and the Central African Forest Initiative.

The Importance of Collaborative Action

The success of saving the Congo Basin's forests depends on working together: Local communities, global partners, and different programs all need to assist. France's new financial plan makes sure the money is spent wisely by intertwining it with clear goals, like reducing deforestation. This approach was inspired by successful projects in Brazil. This adds on to previous agreements like the $500 million deal from COP26 for the DRC's (Democratic Republic of Congo) forests.

Experts on the Congo Basin research communicate that shielding the Congo Basin is not only a solution for weakening climate change but also for advancing ecoclimate justice, ensuring that local and indigenous communities, who rely on these ecosystems and leaders, succeed in the settlement. These initiatives illustrate that preserving vital rainforests is crucial for protecting biodiversity, addressing the climate crisis, and assisting populations living in surrounding conditions through greenery action and diplomacy.

Indigenous Voices and Community Engagement

The Tropical Forests Forever Facility (TFFF) and Forest and Land Tenure Pledge (FLTP) emphasized the role of indigenous voices. It's not just about fighting climate change, but also about providing everyone an opportunity to speak on policies, predominantly by protecting the rights of indigenous people and letting them take part in decisions about their land. According to the COP30 Presidency, over $5.5 billion USD has been declared for the Facility, with 53 countries endorsing this cause.

Norway pledged $3 billion over ten years, France pledged 500 million euros, and the Netherlands offered $5 million to TFFF's Secretariat. Norway reaffirmed support for the target finance management of $1.8 billion for forest governance and secure community lands. Brazilian President Luiz Inácio Lula da Silva has promoted the Tropical Forests Forever Facility (TFFF) as the future of climate finance, insisting its scalable investment model offers a stronger option to contrast traditional grant-based funding. Experts on this research believe that without community engagement, the efforts made toward conservation in the Congo Basin are likely to be unsuccessful.

Community-Centered Approach: $300 million is specifically reserved for projects introduced by Congolese public institutions, ensuring local leadership and alignment with national climate priorities while respecting indigenous rights and knowledge.

Expert Perspectives

Dr. Yadvinder Malhi, Oxford University Ecologist:

"It has about 10,000 plant species and 30% of these can only be found in the region. This fountain of water in the heart of the continent circulates and also ends up feeding into the Nile. That sustains the lives of millions of people."

Arlette Soudan-Nonault, Republic of Congo's Environmental Minister:

"The people of the Congo basin have tightened their belts so that the world can breathe – and we receive no compensation. It's not just an issue of instability. We need to look at ways of creating sustainable economic growth."

Concerns and Challenges

German policy analyst Max Alendar Matthey was concerned about a "Mini-TFFF," with investments from donors being considerably less than $25 billion. "If markets turn the wrong way, donor funds could be gone in an instant, wiping out billions that were initially paid for forest protection," he told Mongabay on a video call. Matthey also warns about the cost structure, taking in many profits. "If returns shrink, TFFF could end up reliably financing only its own fees, not meaningful rainforest payments," he claimed. "At that point, incentives for forest protection simply lose to deforestation drivers."

Short and Long-Term Implications

Short-term results on this initiative would be through structures of partnerships and funding, and the strategizing of implementation. There could be technical concerns, budget constraints, political concerns, and rising pressures of deforestation. Long-term effects could be the neglect of the Congo Basin in global climate policy, challenges in governance, and weakening financial systems. If ignored, the Congo Basin could transition from a carbon sink to a carbon source, with increased climate instability across central and eastern Africa.

Conclusion

The Congo Basin has played a major role in sustainability in Central Africa and regions, for water sources and tropical engagement. Moving forward, it's key to remain updated with climate finance and environmental updates, with management of TFFF and the $25 billion Congo Basin initiative. Without protection, accelerated deforestation could occur, along with biodiversity at risk. International cooperation and diplomacy are needed moving forward to determine the future of this ecosystem.

Looking Ahead: The success of the $2.5 billion initiative depends on sustained international cooperation, meaningful community engagement, and performance-based accountability. The fate of the Congo Basin—and by extension, global climate stability—hangs in the balance.
